Project 1: Project Proposal and User Research

Part 1A: Identify Project Idea

Our project idea is a simple unit converter mobile app, supporting manual input to convert all basic units of measure for length, time, temperature, weight, etc., with additional functionality. Specifically, the user would also be able to take a photo with their phone camera, and our app would retrieve text from the photo as a string, use that string to identify the units used, and convert the units automatically.

This system can be used in many contexts: for example, users may need to convert miles to kilometres on street signs while travelling, cups to fluid ounces in a recipe, inches to centimetres to check knitting gauge, and so on. For our users, we will place the most emphasis on those who may use it more frequently, such as home chefs, craft enthusiasts, frequent travellers, etc. However, ultimately, this system can be used by anyone, since we have all needed to convert some sort of measurement before in our lives.

We aim for this app to be available to our users whenever they need it. More often than not, in our daily lives, we don’t expect to encounter unfamiliar measurements. This is especially true in Canada, where the relatively recent metrification of units has left us understanding some measurements better than others based on context (e.g. distance is almost always in kilometres, but most Canadians know their own height in feet and inches). As such, we intend for this to be a mobile app. This will allow our user base to have the application available to them for use at a moment’s notice, since we now live in a society where smartphones are fairly ubiquitous.

Part 1B: Identify Stakeholders

The primary stakeholders in this instance are the users of the app, however, that includes a broad range of people. For instance, an older person using the app to convert measurements for cooking may be unfamiliar with common app design standards, while a contractor or engineer who may need to make conversions frequently will desire flexibility to make conversions more efficiently or easily. As such, it is critical that our design be simple and easy to read, while incorporating intuitive and easy-to-use shortcuts or customizability.

Additionally, we acknowledge that there may be additional stakeholders who are not users, but may have reasons to be interested in our system. As such, we will consider three main stakeholder groups in our design:

  • The older or more inexperienced user. This application could be very useful to older users, since it will have the capability to perform conversions automatically. Many older individuals are also hobbyists, so our application could appeal to them as well. For these users, it will be important to have a simple interface with clearly labelled UI elements and concise instructions where necessary, in order to foster a simple, hassle-free experience with our system.
  • The younger or more technologically-savvy user. This encompasses most of our target user base of crafters, contractors, bakers, and other hands-on individuals who are already interested in using technology for their projects. We will assume that these users will have some experience using mobile applications, particularly ones with camera functionality (e.g. Snapchat). As such, it will be important to create an aesthetically pleasing interface that accomplishes the task of unit conversion quickly for these users.
  • Other system developers. These could be developers of rival apps (see Secondary Research below), or developers of systems that would benefit from integrating unit conversion. For example, it may be useful for the developer of a recipes application to have a unit converter available. As such, we would need to be mindful of the fact that other developers may be looking at this system with the intent of developing a rival application, or even incorporating our system into their own application. We will assume that these individuals are highly skilled & knowledgeable of system design already - as such, it will be important to conform to industry standards for mobile application design.

Part 2A: User Research - Secondary Research

Justification of Method:

Our team selected secondary research to determine what other similar solutions exist in an attempt to understand what users want from applications in this domain. We believe this method is a good choice because we can gain more useful information from the real marketplace, such as if a currently available application is successful, and if it has design flaws from self exploration of the application, and/or user comments about the application. We can also observe users interacting with those similar products, since we cannot demonstrate designs at this stage of our project for our own designs as we do not have a detailed design for our application yet. Additionally, we chose this method to determine what can realistically be implemented to inform our design choices by explicitly observing what can be done. As aforementioned, we wish for our application to conform to industry standards in order to cater to our third stakeholder group; thus, we are using this investigation method in order to capture ideas for what is being done well, or what can be done better. Furthermore, since this is not a research project, and we are implementing an existing technology, we believe learning from previous attempts to solve the problems associated with this domain is a good place to start. Finally, this methodology allows us to understand the design space in a market context to determine which features the users really want rather than relying on their advice or our own potentially flawed intuition. We believe this method will allow us to develop strong opinions of what design decisions should be made.

Part 2B: Error Analysis

Justification of Method:

The application proposed by our group of a mobile device capable of viewing and interpreting numerical values and their associated units, then converting those units to another unit that is requested by the user, is a currently existing technology available in many applications on the market. While on the other hand, our design is not complete nor available for testing. Therefore, our proposed solution will need to improve upon the currently available solutions to compete in the market.  And accordingly, our solution must be more user friendly and less prone to errors than other solutions to do so.  In order to understand the ways similar applications are used, and misused, we should perform an error analysis of similar applications.  Determining the shortcomings of other methods that are available to solve similar problems will provide us with design insights for our solution. Many of these insights may be related to user experience and user interface design.  Through performing an error analysis of similar applications we hope to improve the usability of our design.

Slips:

  • User mistypes the value
  • Make sure it’s easy to change - backspace and clear buttons
  • User accidently chooses the wrong unit from list
  • Maintain scroll position and highlight current selection to make it easy to find
  • User may accidentally toggle or change options when they mean to interact with some other element of the interface.
  • Ensure that our option menus are placed well, with adequate spacing, and that it is clear what each button is for upon first glance.

Mistakes:

  • User gets an incorrect conversion due to choosing the wrong units
  • Make sure units are clearly displayed - potentially display formula as well
  • User gets an unintended result due to mistakenly pressing the wrong button
  • Make sure buttons are clearly labelled
  • User can’t change settings from their current screen/task location
  • Make menu clearly accessible at all times
  • User might not position camera correctly, causing camera conversion to be unsuccessful or inaccurate
  • Could place guidelines in camera viewfinder for the users to position the text they want to convert

Summary of Research:

It would seem that based on the above research the most important places to focus design attention to are:

  • Maximizing the number of units available for conversion (both input and output), for ease of access.
  • The camera feature should work just as fast or faster for the human user to take out the mobile device and capture the image than just sitting at the nearest computer and typing it into Google.
  • The application should maximize compatibility with Android devices.
  • The application should have a minimal disk, RAM, and CPU usage
  • The application should categorize the units for input and output into temperature, distance, mass, etc. categories.
