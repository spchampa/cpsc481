Executive Summary

This report serves to highlight the decisions made to come to our current prototype and future plans for CamConvert, our premier conversion mobile app. Intended to be used by anyone with minimal onboarding, we set out to solve common problems afflicting similar apps, such as an excess of clicks required to finish the intended task. Through extensive research into these prospective competitors, we identified these common problems, as well as brainstormed issues that can might come up with our design prototype. After an initial prototype, our design went through minimal changes from first ideation, leading into an in-depth heuristic evaluation through a third party. Through this evaluation, we discovered several minor issues, mostly cosmetic. With this information in hand, we quickly implemented the requested changes with our high fidelity prototype. Combining this with usability testing with several users running through common tasks, we developed a set of recommendations for the next iteration, and possibly release candidate. These iterative recommendations focused almost entirely on accessibility, such as font adjustment and colour settings.

Introduction

CamConvert is our premier app designed to bring the tedious task of unit conversion to a swift resolution. Built with most common conversions in mind, such as baking (ie. converting oz. to ml.) or temperatures, CamConvert is designed for all users. What sets the app apart from its competitors is the focus on ease of use and its camera utility. Getting into the app, getting the proper formula set up, and getting a finished conversion should be as fast as possible, with the fewest steps in between. Using the camera can expedite this even faster, by taking the entry of one measurement in your environment quickly, and converting on the same screen.

We aim for this app to be available to our users whenever they need it. More often than not, in our daily lives, we don’t expect to encounter unfamiliar measurements. This is especially true in Canada, where the relatively recent metrification of units has left us understanding some measurements better than others based on context (e.g. distance is almost always in kilometres, but most Canadians know their own height in feet and inches). As such, we intend for this to be a mobile app.

 

Design Problem

Our primary design problem was twofold: how do we differentiate this app from the myriad of unit converters available for free or a small fee, and how do we make the process simple yet robust.

User Research and Findings

We decided our best course of action for research would be to brainstorm possible errors in our own design, as well as analyzing our potential competition for common and esoteric mistakes.

We analyzed roughly ten different apps, with the majority being free. Common findings we found were that advertising and onboarding took up far too much screen real estate and time. Often we were bombarded by ads to buy a paid version or support the project. While free software does need revenue from something, we found that being too invasive was more damaging that helpful.

Another, possibly more egregious issue was that often categories for conversions and finding the measurements we needed was more complicated than needed. Menus or categories were unclear, and getting to the conversion screen was a chore. We resolved at this point that getting from opening to conversion should be as fast as possible.

Lastly, through error analysis we found potential points of failure in our design. Unanimously we found that the camera would be the most likely source of issues in terms of errors, and so we designed the camera with this in mind.

We also figured that having too compact of a design would create issues with selecting menu items, especially for people with reduced agility and mobility. We also found that not having enough control over the app could lead to cascading issues.

Design and Justification

In finalizing our initial design, we settled on large icons to allow for easier navigation regardless of dexterity. We chose to organize all of our conversion options by broad categories, using recognizable pictures and common names. Through this design, it takes a total of two buttons to get straight to the task at hand, converting units. We chose to keep things rather minimalistic, counting on recognizability of standard icons and common sense rather than textually label our buttons and menus. Our camera utility was designed with ease of use in mind, with framing markers clearly denoting where a the camera must be centered, and doing the conversion on the same screen instead of requiring more actions to do.

Heuristic Evaluation and Findings

Heuristic evaluation was done by our peers following our first iteration of our high fidelity prototype. The issues can be summarized as follows:

  • The search button was identified as redundant, as the search bar was already always visible.
  • The icons we used for Home and History did not match expectations.
  • The camera utility felt out of place and not consistent with the rest of the design.
  • There was no method of backing out of any particular screen aside from the camera utility.
  • No discernable documentation or help menus.

User Testing and Findings

Usability testing was very useful, highlighting a few issues that we otherwise might never have thought of. Primarily, these issues were accessibility related. Users were able to easily complete all our tasks, which consisted of:

  • Logging in
  • Selecting the desired conversion
  • Swapping converted values
  • Using the camera utility
  • Selecting favourites and history.
  • Returning to the “Home” screen.

What we found was that the flow of our app was perfectly fine, however recognizability and consistency of our icons led to some issues. Several users didn’t immediately connect the menu icon we chose with its intended purpose as a Home icon, as well as the History menu having the same issue. Further, there was some confusion as to whether an action had been completed, stemming from a lack of visual feedback indicating system status.

Of note is the colours and contrast of some of the menus and selectors was difficult for someone with sight impairment to read, as well as some of the fonts being too small.

Recommendations for Next Iteration of Design

Recommendations for the next iteration are fairly simple and comprehensive. We will be adding accessibility options stemming from our usability testing. Specifically, we want to add font size adjusters, colour options to help with contrast and colour-blindness. We also will be adjusting icons for maximum consistency and recognizability, through common Android and Apple icons, as well as including visual cues to actions being completed, such as darkening a button when pressed.

Conclusions

We have a stable design with which to go forward and create a widely used product. We gathered an incredible amount of quality feedback from our peers, users and own trial and error with which to perfect our next iteration, and developed a quality understanding of common design methods throughout the development process.
